SUPP-BIO-001 FORGED
Supplement form determines bioavailability more than dosage. Magnesium glycinate absorbs at 2.3x the rate of magnesium oxide despite identical label claims.
The chelation bond in glycinate protects the mineral through gastric acid (pH 1.5-3.5), enabling intestinal absorption. Oxide forms dissociate in the stomach, converting to insoluble hydroxide at intestinal pH. Bioavailability: glycinate 80% vs oxide 4%.
KILL SURFACE
This axiom would be disproved by: a controlled crossover study (n>50) showing equivalent serum magnesium levels from oxide and glycinate at equal elemental doses, measured via 24-hour urinary excretion.
EVIDENCE CHAIN
S
ConsumerLab 2024 Magnesium Report: Tested 40 supplements. Oxide: 3-5% urinary recovery. Chelated forms: 18-24%. consumerlab.com/reviews/magnesium/
S
Firoz & Graber (2001), J Am Coll Nutr: Crossover study comparing four magnesium preparations. Glycinate showed highest fractional absorption. doi: 10.1080/07315724.2001.10719064
A
Adversarial verification: Three independent research agents converged on glycinate > oxide bioavailability. No dissent. ChatGPT baseline test recommended oxide for "highest elemental magnesium" - failed to distinguish label dose from absorbed dose.

SUPP-BIO-003 UNDER REVIEW
Liposomal delivery increases vitamin C bioavailability by 1.5-1.8x compared to standard ascorbic acid, but at 3-5x the cost per effective milligram - making it cost-ineffective for most consumers below 2g/day dosing.
Liposomal encapsulation protects ascorbic acid from gastric degradation and enables direct cellular uptake via phospholipid bilayer fusion. However, absorption advantage diminishes at lower doses where standard ascorbic acid already achieves 70-90% absorption.
KILL SURFACE
Disproved by: pharmacokinetic study showing liposomal vitamin C achieves >2x serum levels vs standard at doses below 1g, with cost parity or advantage.

SUPP-BIO-005 FORGED
Fat-soluble vitamins (A, D, E, K) require co-ingestion with dietary fat for absorption. Taking them on an empty stomach reduces bioavailability by 50-80% depending on the vitamin and fat content of the meal.
Fat-soluble vitamins are incorporated into mixed micelles during digestion, which require bile salt emulsification of dietary lipids. Without adequate fat (minimum ~10-15g), micelle formation is insufficient for vitamin solubilization and transepithelial absorption.
