The label lie

When a bottle says "Magnesium 400mg," you'd reasonably assume you're getting 400mg of magnesium. You're not. The form of the mineral determines how much your body actually absorbs, and the difference isn't marginal - it's often 20x.

This isn't a niche biochemistry detail. It's the single most important fact about supplements that most consumers don't know, and that most supplement companies have no incentive to explain.

SUPP-BIO-001 FORGED
Supplement form determines bioavailability more than dosage. Magnesium glycinate absorbs at 2.3x the rate of magnesium oxide despite identical label claims.
Bioavailability: glycinate 80% vs oxide 4%. The chelation bond protects the mineral through gastric acid, enabling intestinal absorption.
Forged Feb 2026 - 2 challenges resolved, axiom stands

The practical implication: if you're taking 400mg of magnesium oxide, you're absorbing roughly 16mg. Switch to 200mg of magnesium glycinate and you'll absorb roughly 160mg - ten times more from half the dose.

Why fat-soluble vitamins need food

Vitamins A, D, E, and K are fat-soluble. This means they require dietary fat to form the mixed micelles that enable absorption. Taking them on an empty stomach - which most people do, with their morning stack - reduces absorption by 50-80%.

SUPP-BIO-005 FORGED
Fat-soluble vitamins (A, D, E, K) require co-ingestion with dietary fat for absorption. Taking them on an empty stomach reduces bioavailability by 50-80%.
Minimum ~10-15g dietary fat needed for micelle formation. Without fat, vitamin solubilization and transepithelial absorption is insufficient.
Forged Feb 2026 - No challenges

The fix is simple: take fat-soluble vitamins with a meal that contains fat. Even a tablespoon of olive oil or a handful of nuts is enough.
